Optimising Qantas Health Insurance

Optimising the Qantas Health Insurance funnel one experiment at a time

Qantas Ventures was set up in part to diversify risk exposure.  It is a core part of the Qantas Loyalty division, which is the largest loyalty program anywhere in the world with around 14m members.

The first venture that they pursued was a partnership with nib to offer Health Insurance to its members, in 2016.  The value proposition was that members could earn Qantas Frequent Flyer (QFF) points, not only on the premiums, but also as a bonus for signing up to the program.  This was a very successful first venture, and over the four years it has also been the fastest growing health fund, as well as enjoying the lowest year-on-year premium increases in the industry.

It had been identified that the application form, or 'funnel', was in need of modernising and optimising - especially to drive traffic to the Sales & Engagement (S&E) call centre in Gosford.  The wanted to embark on a discrete, 6 month time-boxed piece of work to perform Conversion Rate Optimisation on the funnel.  It was found that once a member talked to the S&E team, they were four times more likely to convert to a policy, so they wanted to optimise the opportunities for this to happen.

I was engaged as a both a Senior Experience Designer, and also as the Conversion Rate Optimisation specialist.  Given my very successful optimisation of the Westpac Home Loans application process this seemed a natural fit, and I embarked on visually and experientially designing a series of experiments to run to test hypotheses around why attrition was happening, or why people might need extra help by talking with someone.

The experiments

There was no shortage of experiment candidates

Forming the shortlist

It was important to prioritise the candidates for experiments.  There certainly wasn't a shortage of ideas!  In fact the hardest part of this was staying focused on the end goal, which was to drive lead traffic to the S&E team.  It would have been easy to pick digital enhancements that would have likely led to more digital sales, but this was not the remit of the exercise and we all needed to maintain that focus.

My first experiment was to have contact information in a banner below the main hero area.  Previously this was with less affordance in the header area, where people are susceptible to 'banner blindness'.  My hypothesis was increasing affordance plus compelling iconography would increase leads.  I was right.

How I messaged this to the business

Good news needs to get out

It was made very clear to me how high profile this initiative was.  This was a totally new way of running a project at Qantas Ventures.  It was entirely self-contained and went against all of the normal BAU ways of working.  It also required sign-off not only from Olivia Wirth (CEO, Loyalty) but also Alan Joyce, who is the CEO of Qantas Airways.  No pressure then.

I decided we needed to have a digital wall, that would show experiments in train, as well as the results that had been called.  I wanted to gamify this.  I had always liked the way that Antony Green on the ABC was the focus of the election.  When he said a result was called, it was called.  So I thought it would be interesting to have the resident analytics expert Aravind (or Aras to his friends) be our Antony Green.  Everyone knew him and he thought it would be great fun.

Alongside this were bar charts of the metrics we were measuring against using Google Optimize.  They generally all had leads as a metric, and usually a sales target too, but also things like 'progression to next screen' was important to measure attrition.  These were updated daily and put onto a digital wall in the main thoroughfare of the floor.

Aras calls it.  Not difficult given the third metric...

One experiment took four iterations to win

Login simplification.  Sounds simple.

You'd think so.  Simplify the cognitive load on the login screen, along with the messaging, and more people are going to progress through the funnel. I thought this would be the quickest of quick wins as an experiment. Previously we had a login box on the left, and a continue as guest on the right. This seemed confusing as the both looked similar and we were losing 50% attrition this early in the funnel. So, make it one column, add the fuel that logging in would reduce the fields they needed to complete, reduce the affordance of guest flow and wait for the results to come. Except they didn't. Three further iterations also didn't. Of all the experiments, we learned the most from this one:

  1. Users will take the path of least resistance every time.
    Previously we asked if they were QFF members or not. That always has a truthful binary outcome. Users are unlikely to lie at this point. When we introduced a different heading of 'Would you like to login to save you time?' - We gave them an out and they took it.

  2. Words are confusing
    I thought an explanation about why it was better to take one route than another might help guide them.  I was wrong.  It increased the overall cognitive load again, which was the one thing we were trying to decrease.  In this situation, less is more.

  3. Question the metrics all the time
    I was getting frustrated with the constant ~10% drop in leads on each experiment.  This didn't seem logical.  Until I delved deeper and looked at what constituted a 'lead'.  We weren't taking into account guest path with mobile number entered, nor were we taking people that emailed themselves a quote.  All very valid leads for the S&E team.  When that was taken into account, the drop fell into margin of error territory (~4%).

We all agreed this was the big one to nail, and on our fourth attempt we did.  We used a single column, minimal explanatory text, with the fuel header being 'Login to see your quote'.  By doing this, we increased sales by 32%, which on its own generated $254k lifetime EBIT uplift to the bottom line.

Overall project results

Presenting the results to the Ventures team

Spoiler - we did pretty well

The target that was on the business plan for the project was to ultimately generate $0.5m EBIT (Earnings before Interest and Tax) on annual sales, and $1m EBIT on the lifetime of the policy.  This sounded a daunting number to hit given we only had around 4.5 months of real experimentation time ahead of us, but - we blew that out of the water.  We ended up getting $1.01m annual, and $2.1m lifetime EBIT across the 12 experiments.  Of the 12 experiments, 11 were a success (including simplification at the fourth time of asking), with only one experiment narrowly failing on leads.  This again was one I had high hopes for - displaying call wait times when these were less than three minutes, and hope that this can be iterated again in the same way simplification was, to end up being a complete success.
