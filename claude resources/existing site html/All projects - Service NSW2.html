Service NSW Online Booking System

Transforming the way we communicate with the State Government for over 8 million citizens

Service NSW pre-pandemic was a government department doing Service Design excellently.  They had replaced the old RTA registries with great results.  Just before the pandemic hit, a project was commenced to transform how driving tests were booked online through Service NSW, and I was engaged through Pragmateam to do a full discovery on how this might happen.  What we discovered might transform the way 8 million people interact with government services going forward.

My remit was to investigate how best to replace an end-of-life system for booking driving tests.  I soon realised that although this was a significant problem to solve, there were many more use cases for this kind of booking system.  To start with, we opened it up to other Transport for NSW options, such as maritime licences and knowledge tests.  We realised that there was a large crossover of user journey in all of these.

After this, I started to think even wider.  I zoomed further out.  How could we build a booking system for all 181 transactions that Service NSW handled at that time (I suspect that is much higher post-COVID).  So we build a fully interactive, data-rich HTML interactive prototype to do this.  But that wasn’t the end of me zooming out, but I’ll come to that later.

At this point the target was to build a booking system to handle any transaction that Service NSW would otherwise deal with as a walk-up.  They had an automated system for filtering these at the door, but anecdotally this was so hard to navigate from an IA point of view, that they needed to deploy a Concierge to navigate this on behalf of customers.  In defence of the system, it’s a complicated IA, and has probably evolved considerably since they were RTA. 

A concierge at a Service NSW branch

Validating our hypothesis

So we recruited a bunch of managers from Service Centres on a virtual workshop to do a card sort on where these should now live.  It ended up not being perfect, and there were now several paths to the same result, but seemed a more cohesive IA than before.

Next stage was to validate this.  We ran two rounds of Usability Testing with the general public.  The first cohort was skewed towards regional and rural users (whilst also having metro), and the second cohort was skewed English as a second language (CALD).  The new IA was overwhelmingly validated, with 7/8 of cohort 1 and 6/8 of cohort 2 understanding all IA tasks.

But we weren’t just looking to validate IA.  We also wanted to do some generative research on their interactions with the Service NSW Centre.  What we found was that no-one was confident about how long they would be there, and this was no different from the days of the RTA.  You could be there 10 minutes, or a couple of hours.  This was especially so in rural areas, where people made a special trip to a regional centre such as Dubbo to get things done.  If you were caught up for 2 hours at the SNSW office, that impacted the rest of your day.  The overwhelming validation was that a booking system for appointments at a Service NSW Centre would be very much a good thing.

We proceeded to ‘designing the right thing’.  What did good look like for these kinds of things?  Well, you could look narrow and see how the other Australian States do this, but I was more keen on best in class.  One that stuck out for me was how you book an appointment to get your Apple device fixed at an Apple Store.  Before this, you’d bring your broken thing into a store, with varying results.  Now, you could confidently reserve time to bring your device back to Apple and know that someone would give you a definitive answer on the spot as to what next steps would be.  Although personally the outcome of these conversations have rarely gone in my favour, I’ve always liked the booking process, and that leant in heavily on my design thinking.  I looked at all of the booking types we could face, and realised that a single variable model could cope with this:

Low-fidelity wireframes of the booking flow

From here, we wireframed out a potential journey based on this flow. We did this using Balsamiq Cloud, which allowed stakeholders to have input into the design process. I find Balsamiq still very useful after all these years, as it is a very straightforward tool to use, and stakeholders who are complete novices can collaborate in real time in design collaboration workshops. The resulting flow is shown here.

Zooming out

From here, we built a testable HTML prototype.  We focused on driving tests as that was our original remit, but we also looked at very different use cases too.  We found that all use cases we were aware of could be booked through our user journey. We did a HTML prototype because there were so many permutations of the journey that flat design files were restrictive to stitch together as a meaningful testable journey.

So from here, we defined the original problem, we identified a wider problem, researched customers, and built a workable prototype to solve this problem, and validated that our solution solved this problem.  This prototype served two purposes – to test with customers, but also use to be a stakeholder engagement piece, and this proved spectacularly successful in this case.

This was the point where I pushed the remit of the task.  Instead of this being limited to Service NSW interactions, what about if this was the front door to all NSW Government bookings?  NSW Health and hospital bookings, Department of Education school parent teacher interviews?  NSW Department of Justice jury service appointments?  Again, we raised this with Executive Management, and they embraced the vision.

Long story short, but the first use case of the platform I designed and architected was for Careers NSW.  Not the one I expected but delighted to see that my original wireframes, journeys and hi-fi visuals were implemented almost flawlessly. 

Since then, I'm delighted to see that this has been rolled out to my favourite use case - an appointment at a service centre. I'm a little frustrated that 'General Apppointment' is hidden in the middle of the options given this is the most likely option (and hadn't been widely publicised), however the fact it is now available is a huge step forward for interacting with Service NSW.

Booking types available so far

This is so important for regional NSW residents in particular. Once this expands, this will easily become the most important work of my career.
