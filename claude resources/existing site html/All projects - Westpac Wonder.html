Redesigning
Wonder

Optimising Westpac's flagship home ownership program

Westpac Wonder was initially conceived to rethink the entire home ownership journey.  For too long it had been centred on selling product - how much money can a bank lend a customer in order to buy a house or apartment.  Wonder was all about the life goal of home ownership, not the mechanics of how that could happen.

I joined the Wonder program relatively late in the piece.  The first version of the site had been launched, and wasn't getting the traction they had hoped for.  It was deemed internally that the project had become too large and bloated to be effective.  Tough decisions were made and at the end of the second Agile Program Increment (PI02), the headcount was downsized from 125 to just 22 high-performing people.  This was when I arrived, fresh from redesigning St.George Bank's home loan application form.

Our job in PI03, scheduled as the last in the active development cycle, was to figure out the pain points in the first iteration of the site, and increase form completion and ultimately conversion statistics.  We had just 12 weeks to pull this around.  

My role in the 22 was the now sole UX Designer, and alongside an exceptional Visual Designer, we set about the challenge of why so many people weren't completing the journey.

Analysing user attrition behaviour

The problem

The existing flow was based on a 'hub and spoke' model.  This meant that at the end of each completed section, they would return to a central page and then continue on to the next logical section.  Each spoke may or may not have it's own progress bar for that section.  Some sections were longer than others.  

It was through investigation of page analytics we realised the first problem.

Users were not going further than the first return to the hub.  There was a sharp drop in completions from anything section two onwards.  So we needed to analyse why.  Through user testing we found overwhelmingly that users didn't feel like progress had been made, and they had no indication of the percentage through the process they had travelled, and they had a feeling of 'disorientation'.  A lot of comments focused on 'reward' - they needed to feel like the work they were putting in would result in something tangible for them. 

This was when we realised this was the first problem to solve.

Existing 'hub and spoke' model

How we went about solving the problem

Given the time period we had, it was clear that quick wins were the order of the day. So we set about analysing what needed to be asked, and in what order. In a form as complex as a home loan application, there are a lot of dependencies of information (for example if you're married affects if they need to enter shared living expenses or not). 

 So we ran a workshop with SMEs to define critical path, and tried to keep the content of the original 'blocks' of pages largely as-is, whilst transforming the overall IA.

We usability tested this in regular two week increments using a full high fidelity HTML prototype of the form, refining order, context and copy along the way.  

The solution

In the end we manage to distill what was a 17 page journey into 9 targeted pages, with a  progress bar doubling as a navigation tool prominently at the top of the page.  We changed the IA from hub and spoke to a true linear model.

We put more supporting information in where it was needed.  We removed jargon and unnecessary clutter from the interface.  And this tested very positively indeed.

The linear approach

The increase in conversion and completion were even better than any of us could have hoped for.  We went from a 1.67% completion ratio in PI02 to 9.25% in PI03.  We also had an increase of 290% of successful 'Approval in Principle' home loan applications.  So much so, that they needed to increase the size of the customer service centre to handle the extra load.  We also noted that the 'time to compete' metric had decreased by over half.

Westpac Wonder is still active today, and is in almost exactly the same structure as when I completed my work on the project.  It will soon be replaced by the Customer Service Hub home loans process, which I also had a significant role in shaping post-Wonder.

What were the key takeaways from this project?

First and foremost we learned that users need to be oriented correctly in order to give them the confidence to complete the journey.  A home loan is the most significant financial decision a person will ever make, and even to this day it is unlikely they will complete that journey 100% online.  They need to feel empowered to make decisions with the form alone, but also to be connected to expert support for the whole journey.  An IA that showed real progress to a defined end goal really helped this, as well as them being able to go back and review any part of the completed form at any time.

Although this project was too early for true 'omnichannel' completion techniques (start at home, review and complete at a branch for example), the work we started with Wonder in introducing this concept fed into the later Customer Service Hub project where the challenge of true end-to-end mortgages were first accepted, including online valuations and electronic signatures.  Due to the fact that this CSH project is not live yet, I am unable to show any published work or design thinking from this project yet.  But I will be more than happy to chat in person about that journey.
